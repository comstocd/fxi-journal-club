Opening Case ‚Äî Keep This Patient in Mind
Inpatient Scenario

A 78-year-old woman with paroxysmal AF (CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc 5), ESRD on hemodialysis, and a hospitalization 4 months ago for a GI bleed from a cecal AVM is admitted with aspiration pneumonia. Her apixaban was held at that admission. Hematology recommended indefinite hold. Cardiology now requests a formal anticoagulation recommendation.

  • Who would anticoagulate this patient today? Show of hands.
  • What single factor is most driving your decision ‚Äî the stroke risk, the bleed history, or the renal function?
  • Would your answer change if the GI bleed was 12 months ago instead of 4?
An estimated 3‚Äì4 million Americans meet guideline criteria for anticoagulation in AF but are deemed ineligible due to bleeding risk.

This patient is not an edge case. She is the target population.

What if a drug could change the threshold?
Why Current Anticoagulation Is Incomplete

DOACs Solved Half the Problem

What DOACs Achieved
  • ~50% ICH reduction vs. warfarin
  • No INR monitoring required
  • Predictable pharmacokinetics
  • Reversal agents now available (FXa, IIa)
What DOACs Couldn't Solve
  • Major bleeding: ~2‚Äì4%/year in AF trials
  • GI bleeding elevated vs. warfarin (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, edoxaban)
  • ICH ~0.5‚Äì0.8%/year ‚Äî reduced but not eliminated
  • No safe option for prior ICH, active GI disease, or ESRD
The problem is not drug-specific ‚Äî it is mechanistic. Both FXa and thrombin are required for normal hemostasis. Blocking them reduces thrombosis but necessarily impairs the physiologic response to injury.
Is there a node in the coagulation cascade that contributes predominantly to pathologic thrombosis but is dispensable for normal hemostatic plug formation?

This question launched the FXI inhibitor program.

The Natural Human Experiment

Congenital FXI Deficiency
(Hemophilia C)

Effect on Hemostasis
  • No spontaneous bleeding
  • Normal wound healing
  • Bleeding only with surgery/trauma ‚Äî and only at high-fibrinolytic sites (dental, urologic, nasal)
  • Contrast with hemophilia A/B: spontaneous hemarthroses, life-threatening bleeds
  • Variable phenotype even among homozygotes
Effect on Thrombosis
  • Significantly reduced DVT and PE rates
  • Lower ischemic stroke risk ‚Äî especially cardioembolic
  • Reduced prevalence of AF-related thrombus
  • Supported by Israeli population registry data (Salomon et al., Blood 2008)
  • Effect most pronounced in venous and cardioembolic (stasis-driven) events
Autosomal recessive ¬∑ Common in Ashkenazi Jewish populations (~0.3%) ¬∑ This population data is the entire pharmacological justification for the drug class
FXI inhibition can uncouple thrombosis from hemostasis ‚Äî reducing clot propagation in pathologic contexts driven by stasis and contact activation, while preserving the extrinsic-pathway-driven primary hemostatic response to injury.

This would be a fundamentally different risk profile from any current anticoagulant.
  • Why doesn't hemophilia C cause spontaneous bleeding like hemophilia A?
  • What types of thrombosis do you predict FXI inhibitors work best for ‚Äî arterial, venous, cardioembolic?
Critical Mechanistic Distinction

FXI Inhibition vs.
FXIa Inhibition

FXI Inhibition (Upstream)

Targets the zymogen before activation. Prevents FXI ‚Üí FXIa conversion entirely. Complete upstream blockade of the amplification loop.

Example: Abelacimab (monoclonal antibody)

vs.
FXIa Inhibition (Downstream)

Competes at the active site of the already-activated enzyme. Leaves unactivated FXI available. Partial suppression depending on dose and kinetics.

Examples: Asundexian, Milvexian (small molecules)

This distinction appears to have determined which drug class succeeded in Phase 3 and which failed. Keep it in mind as we walk through the trials.

Trial Narrative
Act I ¬∑ Phase 2 Promise

The Excitement Builds

What Phase 2 Showed
  • FXI/FXIa inhibition consistently reduced VTE in orthopedic prophylaxis vs. LMWH
  • PACIFIC-AF: asundexian 50mg reduced bleeding vs. apixaban with efficacy signal intact
  • Consistent ICH reduction signal across agents
  • Phase 2 excitement was real ‚Äî and warranted investigation
The Cautionary Flags Missed
  • VTE prophylaxis trials used asymptomatic venographic DVT as primary endpoint ‚Äî a surrogate with poor track record
  • Phase 2 AF studies not powered for clinical stroke outcomes
  • Dose-finding for adequate FXIa suppression in stroke prevention was not validated
  • History lesson: ximelagatran had Phase 2 promise too
Act II ¬∑ The Reality Check
OCEANIC-AF

Asundexian vs. Apixaban in AF

Trial Design
  • Double-blind, double-dummy, active-controlled
  • Noninferiority design vs. apixaban
  • CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc ‚â•2 (men) / ‚â•3 (women)
  • ~14,000 patients ¬∑ 41 countries
  • Primary efficacy: stroke or systemic embolism
  • Primary safety: major bleeding (ISTH)
The Result
  • Stopped early by DSMB at interim analysis
  • Asundexian FAILED noninferiority for stroke/SE prevention
  • HR for stroke >1.0, upper CI exceeds pre-specified margin
  • Major bleeding significantly lower with asundexian ‚úì
  • Safety benefit could not offset efficacy failure
Bayer / NEJM‚ÄìCirculation 2024 ¬∑ Presented ESC 2024
Critical Appraisal ‚Äî OCEANIC-AF

What Should We Take From This?

  • Noninferiority design: is it possible asundexian was designed to demonstrate safety while assuming efficacy would hold? Was it set up to fail?
  • The trial was stopped for inferiority ‚Äî not futility, not safety. How does this differ from trials stopped for benefit, and how should it affect interpretation?
  • Asundexian targets FXIa (active enzyme only). Could inadequate suppression of the FXI amplification loop explain the stroke signal? What dose would you test next?
  • Bleeding was reduced. Does this argue for dose optimization, or does it confirm FXIa inhibition is the wrong target for stroke prevention entirely?
Act III ¬∑ The Signal
AZALEA-TIMI 71 ¬∑ Phase 2b

Abelacimab vs. Rivaroxaban in AF

Trial Design
  • Phase 2b ¬∑ Randomized, open-label, blinded adjudication
  • CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc ‚â•3 ¬∑ 1,287 patients ¬∑ median 21 months follow-up
  • Enriched for high-bleeding-risk: prior GI bleed, prior ICH, age ‚â•80, CrCl 30‚Äì50, prior fall, frailty
  • Comparator: rivaroxaban 20mg daily
  • Abelacimab 150mg or 90mg SC monthly (blinded doses)
  • Stopped early by IDMC ‚Äî overwhelming bleeding benefit
Primary Results ‚Äî 150mg Dose
  • Major/CRNM bleeding: HR 0.38 (95% CI 0.24‚Äì0.60) ‚Äî 62% RRR
  • Major bleeding alone: HR 0.33 (95% CI 0.16‚Äì0.66) ‚Äî 67% RRR
  • ARR: ~7.3%/year ¬∑ NNT ~14 over ~1.8 years
  • ICH: Near-eliminated in abelacimab arm
  • Stroke/SE: Non-inferior to rivaroxaban (HR 1.11, 95% CI 0.58‚Äì2.14) ‚Äî underpowered

Critical Appraisal ‚Äî AZALEA-TIMI 71

Pressing the Findings

  • Stopped early for safety benefit. Statistical consequence? How does the winner's curse affect interpretation of HR 0.33?
  • Stroke/SE was non-inferior ‚Äî but the CI is wide (0.58‚Äì2.14) and only 25 total stroke events occurred. The trial was not powered for efficacy. What can and cannot we conclude? What trial would actually answer this?
  • Population enriched for high-bleeding-risk. External validity to a general AF population? Would the NNT look the same in average-risk patients?
  • Open-label design (SC vs. oral). Rivaroxaban patients knew their drug. Any ascertainment bias for bleeding endpoints?
  • Monthly SC injection vs. daily oral. Will real-world adherence differences alter stroke prevention in ways the trial couldn't detect?
Act IV ¬∑ The Unanswered Question
LILAC-TIMI 76 ¬∑ Phase 3 ¬∑ Ongoing

Abelacimab vs. Placebo in AF

What AZALEA Could Not Answer
  • AZALEA proved bleeding reduction vs. rivaroxaban ‚Äî dramatic, consistent, credible
  • AZALEA had only 25 total stroke events ‚Äî cannot determine stroke efficacy
  • Stroke/SE CI 0.58‚Äì2.14: abelacimab could double stroke risk vs. rivaroxaban and we couldn't detect it
  • The core question: does abelacimab actually prevent stroke?
What LILAC-TIMI 76 Will Answer
  • Phase 3 ¬∑ Event-driven ¬∑ Double-blind, placebo-controlled
  • ~1,900 patients deemed unsuitable for any anticoagulation
  • Primary endpoint: ischemic stroke or systemic embolism
  • Abelacimab 150mg SC monthly vs. placebo
  • Results expected: second half of 2026
Until LILAC reports: AZALEA proves abelacimab is safer than rivaroxaban. It does not prove abelacimab prevents stroke. These are not the same statement.

No Reversal Agents.
For Any Agent in This Class.

What Does NOT Work ‚Äî Class-Wide
  • Andexanet alfa ‚Äî designed for FXa inhibitors only, irrelevant here
  • Idarucizumab ‚Äî dabigatran-specific, irrelevant here
  • 4F-PCC (Kcentra) ‚Äî not established; may not overcome mAb-mediated FXI inhibition
  • FFP ‚Äî likely ineffective for abelacimab; possibly partial for small molecules
  • rFVIIa (NovoSeven) ‚Äî theoretical bypass via extrinsic pathway; no clinical evidence
  • Abelacimab t¬Ω = 30 days ‚Äî drug effect persists 3+ months post-dose
For life-threatening bleeding: supportive care + source control is primary. Consult hematology early. Management will be empirical. This must be weighed explicitly before prescribing.
Hospital Medicine Implications

Which Patients Are We Talking About?

Tier 1 ‚Äî Most Compelling
  • AF + prior major GI bleed ‚Äî the AZALEA-TIMI 71 enrichment population; LILAC-TIMI 76 tests abelacimab vs. placebo in this group
  • AF + prior ICH ‚Äî current standard is no anticoagulation; dramatic ICH reduction could be practice-changing
  • AF + ESRD ‚Äî no renal clearance, no dose adjustment uncertainty
Tier 2 ‚Äî High Interest
  • AF + frailty / high fall risk
  • AF + thrombocytopenia (oncology)
  • VTE prophylaxis in high-risk surgical patients
  • Patients who cannot tolerate oral medications
Tier 3 ‚Äî Uncertain
  • Mechanical heart valves ‚Äî likely contraindicated (no data)
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome ‚Äî FXI not primary pathway
  • Cancer-associated thrombosis ‚Äî awaiting trials
  • AF + mild-moderate CKD ‚Äî DOAC options adequate

Six Things to Remember

Take-Home Points

01
The mechanism is real. Hemophilia C proves FXI inhibition reduces thrombosis with minimal spontaneous bleeding. The pharmacological rationale is sound.
02
FXI ‚â† FXIa. OCEANIC-AF's failure and AZALEA-TIMI 71's bleeding signal suggest complete upstream FXI blockade may be mechanistically superior to FXIa active-site competition ‚Äî but stroke efficacy awaits LILAC-TIMI 76.
03
AZALEA-TIMI 71's bleeding data is compelling ‚Äî Phase 3 must confirm. 67% RRR and near-zero ICH vs. rivaroxaban. LILAC-TIMI 76 (abelacimab vs. placebo, results 2026) is the definitive efficacy trial.
04
No reversal agents exist. Class-wide. Non-negotiable consideration for adoption. Abelacimab's 30-day half-life is the most acute problem.
05
Early stopping inflates effect size. NNT 14 and HR 0.33 are likely optimistic. Real-world data will show regression toward the mean.
06
Two trials to watch: LILAC-TIMI 76 and LIBREXIA-AF. LILAC (abelacimab vs. placebo, Ph3) answers stroke efficacy. LIBREXIA (milvexian, FXIa, Ph3) answers whether OCEANIC-AF failure was drug-specific or class-specific.
