02 ¬∑ CONTEXT

Why Current Anticoagulation Is Incomplete

4 minutes ¬∑ Brief framing

DOACs transformed stroke prevention in AF, reducing ICH by ~50% compared to warfarin. Yet bleeding remains the binding constraint on anticoagulation eligibility. The fundamental problem is not drug-specific ‚Äî it is mechanistic: both thrombin (FIIa) and FXa are required for normal hemostasis. Blocking them reduces thrombosis but necessarily also impairs the physiologic clotting response to injury.

The question that launched the FXI inhibitor program: Is there a node in the coagulation cascade that contributes predominantly to pathologic thrombosis but is dispensable for normal hemostatic plug formation?

DOAC Bleeding Burden

  • Major bleeding: ~2‚Äì4%/year with DOACs in AF trials (ARISTOTLE, ROCKET AF, RE-LY)
  • GI hemorrhage rates elevated vs. warfarin with dabigatran, rivaroxaban, edoxaban
  • ICH ~0.5‚Äì0.8%/year ‚Äî reduced vs. warfarin but not eliminated
  • No reversal for ~48h post-dose without andexanet/idarucizumab

Patients Left Behind

  • History of major GI bleed: often indefinite hold despite ongoing AF
  • ESRD: apixaban labeled for HD, others require dose adjustment or are avoided
  • Prior ICH: standard of care in many institutions is no anticoagulation
  • High fall risk, frailty, thrombocytopenia
  • Post-bariatric surgery: absorption uncertain for oral agents
03 ¬∑ MECHANISM

Why Factor XI Is a Uniquely Attractive Target

8 minutes ¬∑ Core teaching
Walk through the cascade graphic carefully. The key conceptual leap ‚Äî FXI amplifies thrombosis but is not required for hemostasis ‚Äî is what the entire drug class rests on. Residents frequently conflate FXI with the rest of the intrinsic pathway. Make them articulate why FXI is different before moving on.

The Coagulation Pathway ‚Äî What Textbooks Get Wrong

Classical coagulation diagrams treat the intrinsic and extrinsic pathways as equivalent parallel routes to thrombin generation. This is misleading for clinical purposes. Hemostasis in vivo is initiated almost entirely by the extrinsic pathway (TF/FVIIa). The intrinsic pathway ‚Äî including FXI ‚Äî plays its dominant role in amplifying and sustaining clot propagation, particularly in venous and cardioembolic thrombosis where blood stasis and contact activation drive pathology.

The Natural Human Experiment: Congenital FXI Deficiency

Hemophilia C (congenital FXI deficiency, autosomal recessive, common in Ashkenazi Jewish populations, prevalence ~0.3%) provides the critical proof of concept. This is the most important pre-clinical observation in the entire FXI inhibitor story:

FXI Deficiency ‚Äî Hemostasis

  • No spontaneous bleeding
  • Normal wound healing
  • Bleeding only with surgery or trauma ‚Äî and predominantly in high-fibrinolytic sites (dental extractions, urologic, nasal)
  • Normal PTT in many heterozygotes; variable bleeding phenotype even among homozygotes
  • Contrast with FVIII/FIX deficiency (hemophilia A/B): spontaneous hemarthroses, life-threatening bleeds

FXI Deficiency ‚Äî Thrombosis

  • Significantly reduced rates of DVT and PE vs. general population
  • Reduced ischemic stroke risk, particularly cardioembolic
  • Reduced prevalence of AF-related thrombus
  • Population data from Israel (Salomon et al.) and registry studies support this protective effect
üîë

The central premise: FXI inhibition can uncouple thrombosis from hemostasis ‚Äî reducing clot propagation in pathologic contexts (stasis, contact activation) while preserving the extrinsic-pathway-driven primary hemostatic response to injury. This would be a fundamentally different risk profile from any current anticoagulant.

Stop and ask residents before advancing
  • If FXI deficiency protects against thrombosis, why don't patients with hemophilia C have spontaneous bleeding like hemophilia A patients?
  • What types of thrombosis do you predict FXI inhibitors would be most effective for ‚Äî arterial, venous, or cardioembolic? Why?
  • What does the variable bleeding phenotype in FXI deficiency tell you about potential individual variation in drug response?
04 ¬∑ PHARMACOLOGY

Agents in Development

5 minutes ¬∑ Overview + critical distinction
‚ö†Ô∏è

Before reviewing agents, establish the critical mechanistic distinction that explains the divergent trial outcomes: FXI inhibition (targeting the zymogen before activation) vs. FXIa inhibition (targeting only the activated enzyme). This is not a trivial distinction ‚Äî it appears to have determined which drug class succeeded in Phase 3 and which failed.

FXI vs. FXIa: Why It Matters

FXI circulates as an inactive zymogen. When activated to FXIa, it amplifies the intrinsic pathway. Drugs targeting FXI itself (monoclonal antibodies, ASOs) prevent any activation, providing complete upstream blockade. Drugs targeting FXIa (small molecules) compete with substrate at the active site but leave unactivated FXI available. The hypothesis ‚Äî later supported by OCEANIC-AF's failure ‚Äî is that FXIa inhibition at tested doses provided insufficient suppression of the amplification loop for cardioembolic stroke prevention.

05 ¬∑ TRIALS

The Trial Narrative: Promise, Failure, and Breakthrough

14 minutes ¬∑ Core journal club critique
This section should feel like a detective story. Present Phase 2 excitement first, then OCEANIC-AF's negative result (the plot twist), then LILAC's success (the resolution). Assign each trial to a different resident pair to summarize in 90 seconds before your deeper critique.
Act I ¬∑ Phase 2 Promise

Early Phase 2 Data ‚Äî The Excitement Begins

The PACIFIC program (asundexian) and early abelacimab studies demonstrated consistent signal: FXI/FXIa inhibition dramatically reduced thrombotic endpoints in orthopedic VTE prophylaxis while bleeding rates trended lower than LMWH comparators. The PACIFIC-AF Phase 2 trial showed asundexian 50mg reduced bleeding vs. apixaban while maintaining efficacy signal ‚Äî fueling enormous optimism for a paradigm-shifting drug class.

The appropriate cautionary notes that should have been flagged: Phase 2 trials in VTE prophylaxis use asymptomatic venographically-detected DVT as the primary endpoint ‚Äî a surrogate that has poorly predicted clinical outcomes in the past (recall the ximelagatran era). Stroke prevention in AF requires direct clinical efficacy demonstration.

Act II ¬∑ The Reality Check ‚Äî OCEANIC-AF

Asundexian vs. Apixaban in AF: A Critical Failure

Design: Randomized, double-blind, active-controlled trial. Asundexian 50mg daily vs. apixaban (standard dosing) in ~14,000 patients with AF and elevated stroke risk. Primary efficacy endpoint: stroke or systemic embolism. Primary safety endpoint: major bleeding (ISTH).

Result: The trial was stopped early after interim analysis demonstrated asundexian was inferior to apixaban for stroke and systemic embolism prevention. While asundexian did reduce bleeding, the magnitude of efficacy failure meant the overall risk-benefit calculation favored apixaban.

Critical Appraisal ‚Äî OCEANIC-AF
  • What does the noninferiority design choice tell you about the trial's hypothesis? Is it possible asundexian was "approved to fail"?
  • OCEANIC-AF was stopped early for inferiority ‚Äî how does this differ methodologically from trials stopped for benefit? Does early stopping distort interpretation here?
  • Given that asundexian targets FXIa (activated enzyme only), what mechanistic explanation could account for inadequate stroke prevention?
  • Does the finding that asundexian did reduce bleeding argue for a revised dose-finding strategy, or is FXIa inhibition the wrong target entirely?

Critical Appraisal ‚Äî LILAC-TIMI 76
  • The trial was stopped early for safety benefit. What is the statistical consequence of early stopping? (Hint: consider the "winner's curse" ‚Äî point estimates of benefit may be inflated when stopped at interim.)
  • The efficacy endpoint (stroke/SE) was non-inferior by confidence interval, but the event rate was very low and the trial was not powered for superiority. What can we and cannot we conclude about stroke prevention?
  • The population was enriched for high-bleeding-risk patients ‚Äî how does this affect external validity to a general AF population?
  • Open-label design: rivaroxaban patients knew their drug. Does this introduce any ascertainment bias for bleeding endpoints?
  • Monthly SC injection vs. daily oral ‚Äî will adherence differences in the real world matter for stroke prevention?
06 ¬∑ REVERSAL

No Reversal Agents ‚Äî The Critical Inpatient Gap

5 minutes ¬∑ Hospitalist focus
‚ö† Inpatient Safety Alert

As of early 2026, no reversal agent exists for any FXI inhibitor. This is a class-wide gap with immediate implications for inpatient management of bleeding complications and urgent surgical procedures.

  • Andexanet alfa (FXa inhibitor reversal) ‚Äî does NOT work for FXI inhibitors
  • Idarucizumab (dabigatran reversal) ‚Äî irrelevant
  • PCC (4F-PCC, Kcentra) ‚Äî not established; may not overcome antibody-mediated FXI inhibition
  • FFP ‚Äî likely ineffective for monoclonal antibody agents; may partially supplement for ASO/small molecule
  • rFVIIa (NovoSeven) ‚Äî theoretical bypass via extrinsic pathway; no clinical evidence in this context
  • Abelacimab: t¬Ω ~30 days ‚Äî drug effect persists ~3+ months post-dose even with reversal

Practical Inpatient Approach (Until Reversal Agents Are Available)

Life-Threatening Bleeding

  • Supportive care, source control (intervention/surgery) is primary
  • Transfusion per massive transfusion protocol
  • Consider rFVIIa as theoretical bypass agent (no FXI needed if extrinsic pathway saturated) ‚Äî discuss with hematology
  • Consult hematology early ‚Äî management will be empirical
  • Document last dose and expected duration of effect

Urgent Surgery

  • Delay elective surgery if possible ‚Äî abelacimab patients may need weeks without dosing
  • Small molecule agents (asundexian, milvexian): shorter t¬Ω (~10‚Äì20h), practical washout possible
  • No validated point-of-care assay for drug levels currently available clinically
  • FXI activity assay can serve as pharmacodynamic marker but is not rapid
  • Coordinate with anesthesia and pharmacy early
üìå

The reversal gap is most acute for abelacimab specifically because of its month-long half-life. A patient presenting with subdural hematoma 3 weeks after their last injection has no established reversal strategy. This must be weighed explicitly when counseling patients about the drug, and must factor into institutional protocols before adoption.

High-Yield Inpatient Populations (Ranked by Immediacy)

Tier 1 ‚Äî Most Compelling

  • AF + prior major GI bleed ‚Äî the core population LILAC enrolled
  • AF + prior ICH ‚Äî current standard is no anticoagulation; dramatic ICH reduction could be practice-changing
  • AF + ESRD/HD ‚Äî no renal clearance, no dose adjustment uncertainty

Tier 2 ‚Äî High Interest

  • AF + frailty / high fall risk
  • AF + thrombocytopenia (oncology patients)
  • VTE prevention in high surgical risk / orthopedic patients
  • Patients who refuse or cannot tolerate oral medications (SC route)

Tier 3 ‚Äî Uncertain

  • Mechanical heart valves ‚Äî no data; likely contraindicated based on DOAC precedent
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome ‚Äî FXI not primary pathway for APLAS thrombosis
  • Cancer-associated thrombosis ‚Äî awaiting specific trials
  • AF + mild-moderate CKD ‚Äî DOAC options exist; incremental benefit uncertain
08 ¬∑ DEBATE

Controversies and Structured Debate

4 minutes ¬∑ Split-room format
Split residents physically ‚Äî half pro early adoption, half skeptical. Give them 60 seconds to caucus, then argue. This works best if you frame the specific motion: "This institution should prioritize enrollment of our high-risk AF patients into FXI inhibitor trials NOW, rather than waiting for FDA approval."
Motion: "FXI inhibitors should be adopted as first-line anticoagulation for high-bleeding-risk AF patients as soon as FDA-approved"

Argue For

  • 67% bleeding reduction is clinically transformative ‚Äî no DOAC has achieved this
  • ICH reduction essentially eliminates the most feared DOAC complication
  • High-bleeding-risk patients currently get NO anticoagulation ‚Äî any stroke reduction is a gain
  • Renal profile superior to all current oral options
  • Monthly dosing may improve adherence vs. daily pills
  • Natural history data from FXI deficiency suggests decades of safety

Argue Against

  • OCEANIC-AF proves the class is not monolithic ‚Äî efficacy is not guaranteed by mechanism
  • LILAC was underpowered for stroke efficacy ‚Äî we don't know if abelacimab actually prevents strokes as well as apixaban
  • No reversal agent: institutional risk management, surgical complications, trauma
  • Trial stopped early for benefit ‚Äî likely inflated effect size
  • Cost and payer coverage uncertain; monthly injections require infrastructure
  • No long-term (>2 year) safety data on FXI suppression at the population level
09 ¬∑ CLOSE

Take-Home Points

3 minutes ¬∑ Summarize + Q&A
01
The mechanism is real. Congenital FXI deficiency in humans proves that FXI inhibition reduces thrombosis with minimal spontaneous bleeding ‚Äî this is the strongest mechanistic rationale of any anticoagulant target since thrombin.
02
Not all FXI inhibitors are equivalent. OCEANIC-AF's failure (asundexian/FXIa) and LILAC's success (abelacimab/FXI) suggest that complete upstream FXI blockade is mechanistically superior to FXIa active-site competition for stroke prevention ‚Äî though dose optimization may still matter.
03
LILAC's bleeding data is practice-changing ‚Äî if it holds. A 67% reduction in major bleeding and near-elimination of ICH would open anticoagulation to millions currently refused. The caveat is that efficacy for stroke prevention is not yet proven at the superiority level.
04
No reversal agents exist. This is a non-negotiable consideration for hospital medicine. Until reversal strategies are established, adoption requires careful patient selection and institutional protocols for bleeding management.
05
Trials stopped early for benefit inflate effect size. LILAC's NNT of 14 and HR 0.33 for major bleeding are likely somewhat optimistic due to early stopping. Real-world data will show regression toward the mean ‚Äî a lesson from every drug class before this one.
06
Your patient population is exactly who this drug was designed for. High-bleeding-risk, ESRD, prior GI bleed, frail, elderly ‚Äî the Southern Tier hospitalist practice is full of patients who currently get no anticoagulation. Watch Phase 3 data and LIBREXIA closely.
üî≠

What to watch: LIBREXIA-AF (milvexian, BMS) results will be the next major datapoint ‚Äî if a second FXIa inhibitor fails, it confirms the FXI (not FXIa) hypothesis and consolidates abelacimab's position. Phase 3 abelacimab VTE treatment data (AZALEA-TIMI 71) will extend the story beyond AF. Reversal agent development is the critical parallel track for clinical adoption.
